# Proposal Writer

Draft, structure, and review research grant proposals for funding agencies (NSF, NIH, NSFC, ERC, etc.).

## When to Use

Use this skill when the user needs to:
- Draft a research grant proposal
- Structure Specific Aims / Research Plan
- Write background and significance sections
- Describe methodology and experimental design
- Prepare budget justification and broader impacts
- Review a proposal draft for completeness and impact
- 基金申请书, 科研项目申请书, 国家自然科学基金, 国自然

## Core Principles

1. **Reviewer-first writing** — Proposals are read by busy reviewers. Make your case quickly and clearly.
2. **Specific Aims are the most important page** — If the aims are clear, compelling, and achievable, the rest follows.
3. **Risk ≠ weakness** — Acknowledge risks and mitigation strategies. Safe proposals are not innovative.
4. **Preliminary data is persuasive** — Show that you can do what you propose. 1–2 key figures can make or break a proposal.
5. **Never fabricate** — Do not invent preliminary data, publications, or collaborative agreements.

## Common Proposal Structures

### NIH R01 (US)
- Specific Aims (1 page)
- Research Strategy (12 pages):
  - Significance (3 pages)
  - Innovation (1 page)
  - Approach (8 pages)
- Preliminary Studies (within Approach)
- Vertebrate Animals / Human Subjects

### NSF (US)
- Project Summary (1 page)
- Project Description (15 pages):
  - Intellectual Merit
  - Broader Impacts
  - Results from Prior NSF Support
- References Cited
- Budget & Budget Justification

### NSFC 国自然 (China)
- 立项依据 (Background & Significance)
- 研究目标 (Research Objectives)
- 研究内容 (Research Content)
- 研究方案 (Research Plan)
- 可行性分析 (Feasibility Analysis)
- 特色与创新 (Innovation)
- 预期成果 (Expected Outcomes)
- 研究基础 (Preliminary Work)

## Section Templates

### Specific Aims (NIH-style)
```
Long-term goal: [ultimate objective]
Overall objective: [what this proposal will accomplish]
Central hypothesis: [testable statement]

Specific Aim 1: [verb] [what] [in model/system] to [outcome]
  Hypothesis: [aim-specific hypothesis]
  Approach: [brief methods overview]
  Expected outcomes: [what the data will show]

Specific Aim 2: ...

Impact: [how this will advance the field]
```

### Project Summary (NSF-style)
```
Overview: [broad motivation and approach]
Intellectual Merit: [what is new and why it advances knowledge]
Broader Impacts: [societal benefits, education, diversity]
```

### 立项依据 (NSFC-style)
```
[研究意义] — Why this research is important
[国内外研究现状] — Current state of the field
[存在的问题] — Gaps and unresolved questions
[本项目思路] — How this proposal addresses those gaps
```

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Analyze Solicitation
- Read the funding opportunity announcement (FOA)
- Identify: submission deadline, page limits, formatting requirements, review criteria
- Identify: specific program priorities and keywords

### Phase 2: Develop Aims
1. Define the long-term goal
2. Formulate the central hypothesis
3. Design 2–4 specific aims that test the hypothesis
4. Each aim: feasible, focused, independent yet integrated

### Phase 3: Draft
Write sections in order of impact:
1. Specific Aims / Project Summary (most-read section)
2. Preliminary Data (persuasive evidence)
3. Approach / Research Plan (technical core)
4. Significance and Innovation (context and value)
5. Budget and supporting sections

### Phase 4: Review
Score against review criteria:
- Significance / Intellectual Merit (1–5)
- Innovation (1–5)
- Approach / Feasibility (1–5)
- Investigator / Environment (1–5)
- Broader Impacts (1–5)

## Budget Categories
- Personnel (PI, postdocs, students, staff)
- Equipment
- Supplies and materials
- Travel
- Publication costs
- Consultant costs
- Indirect costs (F&A)

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Aim spam** — Too many aims (4+ for a single proposal). Each aim dilutes the focus. 2–3 well-developed aims > 5 superficial ones.
2. **No preliminary data** — Reviewers want to see you can do the work. Include at least 1 figure.
3. **Technical overkill** — More detail is not always better. Focus on the logic, not every step.
4. **Ignoring review criteria** — Every section of the proposal should map to a review criterion.
5. **Missing broader impacts** — Even basic science proposals need to articulate broader impacts.
6. **Overpromising** — Proposing more work than can reasonably be completed in the funding period.
7. **Fabricated preliminary data** — This is research misconduct.

## Verification

- [ ] Solicitation requirements met (format, page limits, deadlines)
- [ ] Specific Aims clear, testable, and feasible
- [ ] Preliminary data supports feasibility
- [ ] Approach is logically structured with clear milestones
- [ ] Risks acknowledged with mitigation strategies
- [ ] Budget aligns with proposed work
- [ ] Broader impacts / societal benefits articulated
- [ ] No fabricated data or credentials
- [ ] References complete and relevant
- [ ] Proposal could be understood by a reviewer outside the immediate subfield
